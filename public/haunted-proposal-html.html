HAUNTED

A Smart Home Narrative — By Lazar Mikov
[HEADER IMAGE: Flickering light bulb / haunted house / phone screen]

Concept

What if your home became part of the horror?

Smart homes are now part of everyday life — we use them to control lights, plugs, heating, music, and more, all from our phones or with a voice command. These devices are designed for convenience and comfort, but their connected nature also opens a door to unexpected interactions — and in this case, fear.

Haunted is the first found footage horror experience that interacts directly with your living space. As the story unfolds on screen, the film uses smart plugs, lights, and speakers to trigger real-world effects: lights flicker, devices switch on, eerie sounds emerge from the next room. Through official APIs and temporary user consent, your smart home becomes the haunted set — blurring the line between film and reality in a deeply immersive way.

Smart Home Narrative?

Traditional screen-based stories reach us through sight and sound. Haunted introduces an experimental Smart Home Narrative — storytelling that extends into the physical environment of the viewer. This is not just a film but a prototype of a new genre where connected technologies turn everyday spaces into narrative arenas. From horror to games to music, smart home narrative opens the door to experiences that blur the line between media and life.

As smart homes move from novelty to norm, they will become the natural stage for a new kind of storytelling. Haunted is a pioneer that shows how films can spill out of the screen and into the connected devices around us.

Short Synopsis

Five friends in a house, All misfits. And of course, they try to summon ghosts.

Their tool, an app. Once installed, the door opens.

Are you here? Flicker once for yes, two for no.

And so it does. Lights flicker once. Fear on their faces.

Ruby, the owner of the home, begs them to stop. Her friends laugh it off as they are sure this is just a funny prank app. The round continues with yes and no questions, oddly accurate answers. Until, lights flicker five times. What does that mean?

Are you real? - One flicker.

Will you leave now? - Two flickers.

Ruby deletes the app and ends the night. However, once installed, the door remains open.

The next day, Ruby is gone.

Her parents find the house empty. Bed made. Car in the driveway. Phone on the kitchen counter. No note. No sign of struggle. Just... gone.

The police find footage on her phone. Filmed that night, hours after her friends left. Ruby is alone in her bedroom. Sitting on the bed. Looking at something off-camera.

"I don't understand what you want," she says to the empty room.

The bedside lamp flickers. Once. Twice. Three times.

The footage continues for three more minutes. Just an empty room. A made bed. Stillness.

Viewer lights flicker five times. The ghost is now in your apartment.

Patterns are Language

The film teaches the viewer to read light through repetition and escalation. It starts simple: one flicker means yes, two means no. Characters ask, lights answer, and the film trains the viewer to count instinctively as the lights in their house flicker.

Then this language expands. The film demonstrates that three flickers mean "it sees you," four means "it knows this place," five means "the threshold breaks." But there is more to just counting. Tempo carries emotion: slow, patient pulses signal something calm, while rapid flickers communicate agitation, anger and urgency. The film introduces color as intent—white for neutral communication, blue for cold distance, red for threat. The film conditions the viewer through constant demonstration.

Bloody Mary and Ouija Board for the New Age

Humans have always sought to communicate with the other side. We've chanted into mirrors, spelled messages with wooden planchettes, held séances by candlelight. The tools change, but the impulse remains: we want to know if something is listening.

Haunted is the latest evolution of that ancient ritual. An homage to Bloody Mary's mirror chant and the Ouija board's patient spelling, reimagined for the era of smart homes and app stores.

Ritual-based horror is an enduring narrative structure in the genre. From Candyman to The Ring, from Ringu to It Follows, the most memorable horror stories are built on simple, repeatable rules: say the name five times, watch the tape, pass on the curse.

Style

Haunted is phrased as a found footage film. This accessibility is crucial: it makes the fear feel achievable, replicable, like something that could happen to anyone with a phone and an app. Additionally, it lowers the costs and simplicity of filming, making it possible to achieve in a short time.

At its core, Haunted is an interactive smart home experience that uses found footage as its delivery mechanism. The viewer connects their smart lights through a companion app or Alexa skill, establishing direct communication between the story and their physical space. The film becomes the conductor, orchestrating their devices in real-time. This transforms the viewing experience from passive observation into active participation.

Development Details

How it Works from the Viewer's Side

Viewers begin by visiting the Haunted website or app, where they're guided through a short setup. They connect the smart devices they already own — whether lights, speakers, or smart plugs — using Google Home, Alexa.

A quick test flickers a lamp or plays a sound to confirm everything works. Depending on which devices the viewer has, the experience adapts: someone with a smart speaker might hear whispers from the next room, while someone with connected lights will see them flicker or shift in color. Each home becomes its own haunted set, uniquely shaped by the devices available.

Then, they press play. The film runs like a normal found footage horror story, but at key moments the viewer's real devices respond — a fan turns on, lights pulse, or a hidden voice creeps in.

The experience prioritises simplicity, ensuring that setup takes only a few taps for a smooth start.

Heavy Lifting and Feasibility

Open APIs and Platforms

Haunted is built entirely on publicly available, well-documented APIs. No proprietary technology or custom hardware is required:

  • Amazon Alexa Skills Kit: developer.amazon.com/alexa/alexa-skills-kit
  • Google Home Actions: developers.google.com/assistant
  • IFTTT Platform: ifttt.com/explore

These platforms allow the film to communicate with viewers' existing smart devices—triggering lights to flicker, speakers to emit sounds, plugs to switch states—all synchronized to narrative moments in the story.

Current Status: Working Alexa Prototype

A functional prototype has been developed over the past month, demonstrating the core interactive mechanics. The system consists of three interconnected components:

1. The Web Platform: https://haunted-production.up.railway.app/

A custom website hosts the video content and serves as the control center. Built using standard web technologies and the site allows users to stream the film directly in their browser, access setup instructions for connecting their devices, and monitor which smart home platform they're using.

The website includes timestamp markers embedded in the video timeline. At specific narrative moments or when the lights should flicker five times—the website sends signals to the connected smart home ecosystem.

At the moment the film is paired to a found footage film that was also shot last month, meant to immerse the viewer. This film is still not a narrative story that was described above but a visual teaser of how the story can feel.

2. The Alexa Skill

Development focused on Alexa's routine and automation capabilities. The skill was built using Amazon's Alexa Skills Kit, which provides device discovery to identify which smart lights/plugs the user has connected, action execution to control those devices, and three virtual sensors to connect the devices to.

How the workflow operates: Users create Alexa routines with the sensors in the Alexa app before watching. At this point the user has to manually create the routines and pre-made routines will be the next step for a user friendly process with less friction.

3. The Synchronization Bridge

The challenge was connecting the web-based video player to Alexa's sensor ecosystem. The current implementation uses a timestamp-based trigger system:

The web player monitors the video's playback time. At specific timestamps throughout the video, the website sends HTTP API requests to toggle virtual sensors between open and closed states. These sensors are connected to the user's Alexa account and monitored by pre-configured routines.

The flow works as follows: Video reaches a predetermined timestamp → Website sends API request to change sensor state → Alexa detects the sensor state change → Alexa executes the corresponding routine (e.g., "Flash lights once") → Viewer's light flickers

Since there is no narrative content yet—only a technical proof-of-concept—the timestamps are simple markers for testing flicker patterns. At these predetermined moments, lights flicker to demonstrate the synchronization works. Future development will align these timestamps with actual story beats (character dialogue, dramatic moments, climactic scenes).

Distribution

Haunted will be distributed primarily through a dedicated web platform and companion app/skill, allowing users to stream the experience directly in their homes. Viewers connect their smart devices through the app, watch the film via browser, and experience synced real-world scares. This approach makes the experience universally accessible — anyone can join in from anywhere, at any time, without needing special hardware beyond the devices they already own.

For viewers who don't yet have a smart home setup but still want the full interactive experience, Haunted will offer optional bundles. These "Haunted Kits" include a small set of plug-and-play devices such as a smart bulb, a smart plug, and a compact speaker. The bundle comes pre-configured to connect with the Haunted app, so setup is as simple as plugging in the devices and linking them once. This ensures that anyone — regardless of their current smart home equipment — can take part in the interactive version of the film.

The project will launch around Halloween, tapping into the seasonal appetite for immersive scares and horror experiences. Haunted positions itself as a refreshing Halloween event for the digital age — turning any connected home into a real-life haunted house.

Alongside its commercial release, the project will be submitted to festivals that nurture new media and immersive content.

Competitor Analysis

Haunted Houses and Attractions

The haunted attraction industry thrives on immersion and atmosphere, generating millions of visitors annually. Yet these experiences are limited by geography, capacity, and seasonality. Haunted takes the appeal of the haunted house — physical, personal scares — and scales it globally by using smart homes as the stage. This makes the experience more accessible (no travel, no queues), more scalable (no fixed venue required), and infinitely adaptable (new seasonal editions or cultural variations can be rolled out worldwide).

Digital Interactive Projects

Interactive horror experiences like Take This Lollipop 2 and Black Mirror: Bandersnatch have shown that audiences are eager for formats that blur the line between storytelling and technology. Their innovation lies in using interactivity or personalization to heighten immersion. Haunted takes this one step further: rather than keeping the interaction on-screen, it brings it into the viewer's physical space. Devices become narrative tools, creating a uniquely embodied form of storytelling that differentiates Haunted from earlier interactive experiments.

Found Footage Horror

The found footage genre has proven to be one of the most effective models for horror storytelling. Projects like The Blair Witch Project (1999) and Paranormal Activity (2007) succeeded by combining minimal production costs with maximum psychological impact. Their power came from intimacy, realism, and the unsettling feeling that the story could be happening "next door." Haunted builds on these same qualities but updates them for a digital-native audience.

Found footage storytelling makes horror feel close to everyday life. Haunted attractions make it tangible. Haunted combines both traditions with digital innovation, creating a scalable, immersive horror experience that lives in the most familiar place — at home.

Rights Clearance & Alexa Skill Certification

Software & Platform Access

All tools used in Haunted are publicly available and free to access. Amazon Alexa's development platform, APIs, and documentation are open to developers worldwide without licensing fees or copyright restrictions. The same applies to Google Home's development tools. This accessibility is by design—Amazon and Google actively encourage third-party developers to build on their platforms.

The skill leverages custom user routines, meaning viewers create their own configurations connecting sensors to their devices. This gives users control over their experience while keeping the system flexible and compliant with platform guidelines.

Alexa Skill Certification

The path to public release is straightforward. Amazon provides clear certification guidelines, and the process is standard for all Alexa skills:

  • Beta Testing: Closed testing with invited users to refine functionality and gather feedback
  • Certification Submission: The skill is submitted through Amazon's Developer Console with documentation and privacy policies
  • Amazon Review: Amazon evaluates functionality, user experience, and policy compliance—a standard review process all skills undergo. This can be only done once the skill is fully developed.
  • Public Release: Once approved, the skill becomes available in the Alexa Skills store

This is an established, well-documented process. Thousands of developers have successfully published Alexa skills following the same path. The tools are accessible, the guidelines are clear, and Amazon provides support throughout development and certification.

Alternative Delivery Methods

Standalone App

If the Alexa skill certification process encounters delays or obstacles, a standalone mobile application serves as an equally viable delivery method. The app would provide the same core experience—synchronized device control and interactive horror—while bypassing the need for Amazon's approval process.

A dedicated app offers several advantages: Direct control over the user experience and timing, cross-platform compatibility (works with both Alexa and Google Home devices), enhanced features like push notifications, in-app tutorials, and visual feedback, no dependency on third-party certification timelines, and ability to update and iterate quickly based on user feedback.

Development of the app is already being explored as a parallel track, positioned either as the primary delivery method or as a complement to the Alexa skill.

The app is built using open-source libraries registered under the MIT License, ensuring legal compliance and transparency in the codebase. Example libraries include:

  • TinyTuya (https://github.com/jasonacox/tinytuya) - For Tuya-compatible smart devices
  • Tapo (https://github.com/mihai-dinculescu/tapo) - For TP-Link Tapo lights and plugs
  • phue (https://github.com/studioimaginaire/phue) - For Philips Hue lighting systems

All three libraries are open-source (MIT License), actively maintained, and widely used in smart home automation projects that control lights locally.

Setup Workflow

1. Configure Smart Lights

  • Add your smart lights to the provided dummy Alexa account (or your own account if approved)
  • Ensure lights are discoverable and controllable via the Alexa app

2. Refresh the Skill

  • In the Alexa app, go to the Haunted skill
  • Disable the skill, then re-enable it
  • This refreshes the connection between the skill and your newly configured lights

3. Find the Three Sensors

In your Alexa app, locate these three sensors:

  • Haunted On
  • Haunted Off
  • Red Sensor

4. Create Alexa Routines

Set up the following three routines:

Routine 1:

  • When: Haunted On sensor triggers
  • Action: All lights → Power → On

Routine 2:

  • When: Haunted Off sensor triggers
  • Action: All lights → Power → Off

Routine 3:

  • When: Red Sensor triggers
  • Action: All lights → Flash red

5. Prepare for Testing

  • Ensure lights are turned on at the physical switch (not just via app)
  • Go to the Haunted website: https://haunted-production.up.railway.app/
  • Click play
